ABSTRACT
This project presents a comprehensive deep learning framework for improved pattern mining and semantic segmentation of 3D point cloud data across diverse domains. With the increasing adoption of LiDAR, photogrammetry, and depth sensors, point clouds have emerged as a critical data format for representing complex real-world environments in detail. However, the inherently unstructured and noisy nature of point cloud data poses significant challenges for traditional processing techniques, particularly in applications such as heritage building digitization, urban scene analysis, and autonomous vehicle perception.
To address these challenges, the proposed framework leverages advanced graph-based and voxel-based neural network architectures, integrating methods like dynamic graph convolution, kernel point convolution, and cross-attention fusion. The system is designed to handle large-scale, heterogeneous datasets by incorporating robust preprocessing, data augmentation, and intelligent sampling strategies. Specialized modules within the framework target key tasks—including boundary-aware part segmentation, autonomous sensor semantic segmentation, urban object reconstruction, and unsupervised anomaly detection.
Experimentation on benchmark datasets validates the framework’s effectiveness, demonstrating superior performance in metrics such as mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) and overall accuracy (OA) compared to conventional approaches. The unified, web-based interface ensures user-friendly operation, supporting both automated inference and interactive exploration of segmentation and anomaly results. This work provides a scalable and interpretable solution for 3D spatial data analysis, with promising implications for digital heritage preservation, smart city development, and the advancement of intelligent autonomous systems.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
3D point clouds are collections of data points defined in a three-dimensional space, commonly used to represent the surface geometry of physical objects and environments with great spatial detail. Generated primarily through advanced sensing technologies such as LiDAR, photogrammetry, and depth cameras, each point in a cloud consists of precise spatial coordinates, and may include additional attributes like color or surface normals. These datasets have become essential in fields such as computer vision, robotics, autonomous vehicles, cultural heritage conservation, and smart city development, as they allow for the accurate digital reconstruction, mapping, and analysis of complex real-world scenes. Unlike regular 2D images or 3D meshes, point clouds are unorganized and unordered, presenting unique computational and algorithmic challenges for processing and interpretation.
1.1 ABOUT THE PROJECT
This project presents a novel deep learning framework using an unified web interface designed for advanced segmentation, pattern mining, and anomaly detection on 3D point cloud datasets. The system is tailored to address pressing challenges in heritage building digitization, urban environment modeling, and autonomous sensory perception. By seamlessly integrating state-of-the-art graph-based, voxel-based, and end-to-end neural network architectures, the proposed solution overcomes the inherent limitations of manual and rule-based techniques, specifically in handling complex real-world data marked by variability, noise, occlusion, and scale. Innovative approaches in preprocessing, data augmentation, and multi-level feature extraction further enhance the framework's interpretability, scalability, and accuracy, resulting in reliable performance on diverse benchmark datasets and supporting robust deployment across both academic and industrial applications.

CHAPTER 2
LITERATURE SURVEY
2.1 GRAPH-BASED SEGMENTATION APPROACHES
Graph-based segmentation networks leverage structural relationships among points to extract local and global features for 3D point cloud analysis. EdgeConv, Dynamic Graph CNNs (DGCNN), and advances like Hybrid Graph-Field Networks (HKGFNet) deliver high segmentation accuracy by constructing k-NN graphs at each network layer and aggregating neighborhood information (Zhou et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024). Heritage building datasets with multi-class part labels (e.g., arch, column, stair) have demonstrated that HKGFNet's bidirectional cross-attention fusion preserves part boundaries more effectively, boosting mIoU to 0.8477 while surpassing point-based and kernel convolution baselines in fine-grained heritage part segmentation.
2.2 TRANSFORMER AND CLUSTERING APPROACHES
Recent transformer-based architectures address point cloud segmentation by dynamically aggregating local and global contexts (Lu et al., 2024). The Adaptive Spatial Structure Graph Transformer (ASGFormer) and Dynamic Clustering Transformer Network (DCTNet) utilize dynamic sampling and spatial graph attention to efficiently model neighborhood relations, achieving high mIoU on urban, indoor, and multi-modal datasets (Han et al., 2024). In the SemanticKITTI and ShapeNet datasets, these mechanisms enhance robustness to occlusion and noisy scans, supporting real-time semantic segmentation for autonomous vehicles and SCANet environments.
2.3 BOUNDARY AND ANOMALY DETECTION METHODS
Boundary-aware models and autoencoder techniques play a critical role in capturing structural details and defects within complex 3D scans. Algorithms like boundary point detection (Mineo & Pierce, 2019), and multi-layer perceptron classifiers using local statistics (Tang & Yang, 2023), focus on delineating sharp edges and detecting outlier regions in architectural and manufactured objects. The use of reconstruction error from autoencoders, coupled with confidence scoring, enables accurate unsupervised anomaly detection in datasets such as MVTec 3D-AD, reaching defect localization accuracies above 93%.
2.4 VOXEL, MESH, AND MULTI-MODAL FUSION METHODS
Voxel-based transformer networks (Zheng et al., 2025), mesh reconstruction, and multi-modal fusion (Zhu et al., 2024) form a growing theme for managing large-scale urban datasets and fusing point clouds with 2D image information. Models like VoxT-GNN combine voxel-level aggregation and flexible receptive field tuning to optimize large-scale 3D scene analysis while U-Next and multi-frame image fusion methods reduce annotation requirements for real-world MLS point cloud datasets. The integration of geometric, radiometric, and image-derived features boosts segmentation and object detection accuracy in challenging urban and infrastructure settings.

SYSTEM DESIGN
This chapter focuses on the architectural blueprint and methodological strategies employed to realize robust 3D point cloud analysis for multiple domains. 
3.1 OVERALL SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
The proposed system architecture is designed as a multi-module deep learning pipeline that ingests raw 3D point clouds and processes them through a series of specialized neural network modules, each tailored for distinct application domains—heritage building segmentation, autonomous sensor semantic segmentation, anomaly detection, and urban scene analysis. The overall workflow emphasizes preprocessing, adaptive model selection, domain-specific optimization.
Fig 3.1: Overall System Architecture Diagram
The proposed framework comprises four specialized modules, each designed to address critical 3D point cloud applications across diverse domains. Below are succinct overviews for each module, highlighting their importance and the technical tasks performed.
3.2 HERITAGE BUILDING SEGMENTATION
Heritage building segmentation focuses on the precise identification and labeling of architectural components in high-resolution 3D scans of cultural monuments. Accurate part segmentation is crucial for conservation, structural assessment, and digital archiving. The module leverages HKGFNet, a novel dual-branch architecture integrating dynamic graph convolution (EdgeConv) and kernel point convolution (KPConv), fused via bidirectional cross-attention gating. This approach excels at preserving sharp boundaries between features such as arches, columns, domes, and walls, handling complex geometries and detail-rich heritage data. Mathematical techniques include graph construction (kk-NN), kernel-weighted convolutions, and label-smoothed cross-entropy/Lovasz loss optimization for multi-part classification.
3.3 AUTONOMOUS SENSOR SEGMENTATION
This module targets semantic segmentation for point clouds acquired from autonomous vehicles and robots, typically from LiDAR sensors in dynamic urban environments. Robust segmentation enables real-time detection of objects (cars, pedestrians, vegetation, roadways) for safe navigation and scene understanding. The Adaptive CylinderNet architecture partitions point clouds using dynamic cylindrical voxel grids—finer granularity for dense, near-field points and coarser for distant regions, thereby maintaining computational efficiency and spatial accuracy. The model applies data-driven voxel-size adaptation, sparse 3D convolutions, and multi-scale feature fusion, with metrics assessed via mean Intersection over Union (mIoUmIoU), Overall Accuracy (OA), and confusion matrices.
3.4 ANOMALY DETECTION
The HKGFNet model (figure 3.2) automates the detection of anomalies in 3D road point cloud data using a hybrid geometric–field learning approach. The model begins with preprocessing and normalization of the raw data, followed by conversion into tensors of shape [B, N, 3]. The feature embedding stage projects XYZ coordinates into a higher-dimensional space for richer geometric representation. In the graph stage, local spatial features are extracted using k-nearest neighbor graphs, while the field stage encodes continuous spatial variations through kernel-based representations. These outputs are fused to enable hybrid feature learning, and the process is repeated hierarchically for multi-scale feature extraction. A bottleneck layer then compresses the learned features into a compact 256-dimensional vector, capturing both local and global spatial information. Finally, the classification head predicts point-wise class probabilities to localize surface anomalies, producing color-coded 3D visualizations and labeled .ply or .csv files for analysis.
3.5 URBAN SCENE ANALYSIS
    The urban scene analysis module facilitates large-scale semantic segmentation of complex city environments—distinguishing buildings, vegetation, roads, and other urban features. Accurate labeling supports applications in city planning, digital twins, and environmental monitoring. The system uses deep architectures such as RandLA-Net and PointNet, deploying hierarchical feature aggregation and mesh reconstruction techniques (Poisson/Alpha/RBF) for detailed visualization. This module includes adaptive data tiling, advanced sampling, and mathematical mesh approximation, enabling detailed part selection, label-based querying, and holistic visual analytics. Evaluation is based on per-point accuracy, class-wise IoU, and geometric reconstruction metrics such as Chamfer/Hausdorff distance.
3.6 DATASET OVERVIEW
    The datasets utilized for this project span four distinct domains of 3D point cloud analysis: heritage buildings, autonomous sensor scans, anomaly detection objects, and large-scale urban scenes.​
Heritage Building Dataset:
    This dataset comprises high-resolution LiDAR scans of historical structures, annotated into ten architectural classes including arch, column, moulding, floor, door/window, wall, stair, vault, roof, and other features. Each sample consists of millions of (x, y, z) spatial points, enriched with additional attributes such as normal components and color intensities. The segmentation task uses these detailed semantic labels to facilitate precise part-wise classification important for digital restoration, documentation, and structural analysis. The data is split into training, validation, and test subsets, ensuring robust evaluation of generalization on unseen sites.
SemanticKITTI Dataset:
    The SemanticKITTI dataset provides point clouds captured from moving platforms in urban environments, representing dynamic elements like vehicles, pedestrians, foliage, and urban infrastructure. Annotated semantic classes allow for effective training and benchmarking of segmentation models aimed at autonomous driving and robotics perception. Notable attributes include timestamped spatial coordinates and intensity values, with diverse scans from variable weather and lighting conditions. The dataset's complexity and scale support rigorous testing of models' ability to handle occlusion, noise, and variability.
Anomaly Detection Objects:
   	For the anomaly detection domain, the project employs datasets featuring various structural anomalies – such as potholes, surface damage, and deformations – with accurate ground truth labels for anomalous regions. Attribute vectors include shape geometry, color, and intensity information. Preprocessing involves normalization, downsampling, and augmentation techniques to enhance the robustness and accuracy of detection models.
Urban Scene Datasets:
    Urban modeling leverages large outdoor point clouds sourced from city-level LiDAR surveys and digital twins. Major datasets like Toronto-3D and customized urban scans provide dense geometric descriptions of roads, buildings, green spaces, and urban fixtures. Data organization follows grid-based tiling and adaptive clustering strategies to enable scalable city-scale object detection and reconstruction. Tasks include extracting topological features and optimizing processing pipelines for massive data volumes.
Each dataset’s points typically store a rich set of features: spatial coordinates (x, y, z), color intensities (r, g, b), surface normals (nx, ny, nz), curvature, depth, and classification labels. Data are loaded from standard formats (.npz, .ply, .txt), preprocessed for error handling and missing values, and visualized using advanced scatter and mesh construction techniques.

The training pipeline is carefully engineered for each module, optimizing deep learning performance while ensuring generalizability and efficiency across datasets.
Training Pipeline Steps:
Data Loading & Preprocessing:
Preprocessed batches are loaded from formated files (e.g., .npz, .ply) into model-ready tensors. Dataloader workers apply real-time augmentation (rotation, jitter, scaling) and normalization. For large datasets, tiling and adaptive sampling strategies are used.
Batching:
Fixed-size batch sampling (e.g., B×N×F, for batch size B, N points, F features). Sampling is randomized to prevent order bias.
Model Training:

Segmentation/Classification:

Optimizer & Scheduling:

Checkpointing & Validation:
Weights are periodically saved, with validation after each epoch on held-out split; the best models are selected by highest validation metric.
